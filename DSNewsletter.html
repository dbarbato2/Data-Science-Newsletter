Leveraging data to link risk and the real estate market
The team has been busy examining the Multiple Listing Service (MLS) data purchased from data aggregator Attom early in 2020 to see what elements might be able to help us further our pricing and underwriting segmentation. While the data has anticipated uses ranging from pre-fill to proactively monitoring one's book of business for any properties going on the market, it turns out there may be other insights to be found. Working with Product Operations, Tanner Kulbashian was able to dig into the data to try and identify pockets of Homesite business linked to the MLS data that was particularly unprofitable. By using classification techniques, there were 2 distinct groups identified. The first is a larger set of properties where the home was either recently sold under a short sale or was real estate owned (REO) when sold. While this cohort makes up a small percentage of homes sold on the market, average loss ratios range between 10 and 20 points above the average Homesite risk. The second cohort is a much smaller but even riskier group of properties. These include homes where the recent sale price was below $30K as well as those whose sale price was less than halved since the previous time they sold. Due to the relatively smaller size of this cohort and the fact that they are experiencing loss ratios exceeding 50 points above the average risk, investigations are underway to determine how the business can be declined going forward. Over time all of these fields will be tested and incorporated into multivariate models such as our inspection and QUEST underwriting models, but we look forward to addressing these risks and leveraging this important data as soon as possible.
American Family Data and the Homesite Rating Plan: What can we learn?
 
Part of American Family’s Property Pricing Development team’s responsibility is to deliver modeled indications for American Family’s by peril HO3, HO4, and HO6 rating plans. With the restructure fast approaching, we determined that the last efforts of the combined team would focus on Enterprise 1 Platform (E1P). Following a round of data preparation, we were able to complete an extensive comparison between American Family’s by-peril rating plan and Homesite’s Mosaic plan, which is the base for E1P. What are some of the key differences we found? First, the peril structure between the two plans is somewhat different. For example, American Family had not yet modeled Lightning as its own separate peril prior to this exercise. We also created variables to mimic Homesite’s claim rating for historical property and auto claims which are easier to use from a modeling standpoint. We adjusted dwelling information to match, removing percent of basement finished in favor of square footage. We did retain some variables such as location of washing machine and customer late payments that don’t exist in the current Homesite plan, but could be added to E1P. We have hope that variables that were not able to be included due to a lack of statistical significance will look better when we can model on enterprise property data. In the future, the Property COE Data Science group will be able to leverage knowledge and data from across the enterprise to create rating plans more sophisticated than any one operating company has currently. A prototype AI model that teaches itself.... subrogation
 
As part of Homesite's recent migration to Guidewire's ClaimCenter, the Data Science team needed to make sure that the existing claims models identifying subrogation and fraud likelihood would still continue to function. The end goal, however, was not just functionality but a more complete integration of the model into the claims system. Toward that end, we wanted to remove model reliance on Homesite-specific predictors that would allow the model to be called from within ClaimCenter and to be tested on other operating companies data as well. The question was, how do we remove those dozens of predictors and still maintain accurate predictions? The answer was to use more advanced Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques. While these models have always leveraged NLP techniques to mine the unstructured text found in our claims notes, Rajat Handa stepped it up with use of a state-of-the-art technique recently open-sourced by Google called BERT. The results were even better than we would have expected and it turns out that with the newer approach, we can not only maintain the same level of predictive power, but we can actually improve upon it. While he was at it, Rajat experimented with some approaches for creating a continuous learner, which are AI models that re-train themselves on some defined cadence. Creating models that are continuous learners allow for improved scalability and increased adaptability to changing patterns in the data. This model will not only improve subrogation predictions but also serve as a prototype for future models where we can experiment with this approach. With proper monitoring and oversight in place, there is an opportunity to have any of our non-filed models utilize this cloud-native autonomous learning framework in the future. Keeping a close eye on all of our AI models
 
One of the most important and often overlooked aspects of building predictive models is making sure that they continue to function both effectively and as expected long after the building and deployment phases are over. We know this is a critical step in the life cycle of a model and have been investing time and resources into developing a solution that can serve as the foundation for all of our monitoring activities. The team has been hard at work developing repositories of model scores that update on a daily basis. This means that users can go into standard SQL databases and determine how each model is performing as well as understand how a model would have scored a particular policy or quote if it had been in production at the time. The retention scores have already been integrated into regular data feeds to GEICO, prioritizing the risk level on renewing policies and we're exploring several other exciting use cases. Please reach out to Heema Pancholi or Khushboo Sah with any questions and view the documentation page for the growing library of available scores.
